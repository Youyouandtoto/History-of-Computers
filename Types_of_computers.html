Caracteristics of modern Computers




I. The First Generation of Computer

The first generation of computers, also known as the vacuum tube era, spanned from the 1950s to the 1960s. This period saw the introduction of the first electronic computers, which used vacuum tubes to process electronic data. These computers were relatively large and expensive, but they were incredibly powerful and capable of performing complex calculations. During this period, computers were used primarily for scientific and military purposes. Some of the most notable computers from this era include ENIAC, UNIVAC, the "Bombs" that allow Turing to crack Enigma's code or IBM 704.

II. The Second Generation of Computers

The second generation of computers began in the late 1950s and lasted until the early 1970s. This era saw the introduction of transistors, which replaced the vacuum tubes of the first generation. Transistors were more reliable, efficient, and smaller than vacuum tubes, allowing computers to become more compact and cost-effective. This generation of computers also saw advances in programming languages. This era also marked the emergence of computers in business, finance, and other industries. Some of the notable computers from this era include IBM 1401, IBM 1620, and PDP-8.

III. The Third Generation of Computers

The third generation of computers began in the late 1960s and lasted until the early 1980s. This era saw the introduction of integrated circuits (ICs) and large scale integration (LSI). These advancements made computers even more powerful, smaller, and cheaper. This generation of computers was also the first to use operating systems, such as UNIX, MS-DOS, and CP/M. This era also saw the emergence of the personal computer, which revolutionized the way people used computers. Some of the notable computers from this era include the IBM System/360, Apple II, Cray-1, and Commodore PET.

IV. The Fourth Generation of Computers

The fourth generation of computers began in the early 1980s and lasted until the early 1990s. This era saw the widespread use of microprocessors and personal computers. This generation of computers was characterized by the emergence of powerful and affordable desktop computers, such as the IBM PC, Apple Macintosh, and Commodore Amiga. This era also saw the introduction of graphical user interfaces (GUIs), which revolutionized the way people interacted with computers. Some of the notable computers from this era include the IBM PC, Apple Macintosh, Commodore Amiga, and NeXT Computer.
